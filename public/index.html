Safety Score Guide

How Yield Scout calculates the 0–100 dividend safety score — and what each factor means for your investment.

🧮
How it works
Five factors, each worth a maximum number of points, totalling 100.
Every stock is scored across five independent factors. The points are added together to produce a final score between 0 and 100. Missing data is never penalised to zero — unknown fields receive partial credit so a single data gap doesn't unfairly sink an otherwise strong stock.
Safe
70 – 100
Strong fundamentals across most factors. Dividend is well-supported.
Moderate
40 – 69
Decent yield but one or more factors show elevated risk.
Risky
0 – 39
Multiple warning signs. Dividend cut is possible.
📈
1. Dividend Yield
Worth up to 25 points
Dividend yield is the annual dividend divided by the stock price. A yield that is too low offers little income value; a yield that is too high often signals the market expects a dividend cut — so the ideal range is 3% to 8%.
Yield RangePointsReasoning
3% – 8%25 ptsSweet spot — strong income, sustainable payout
8% – 12%15 ptsElevated — possible risk, worth investigating
> 12%5 ptsDanger zone — market may be pricing in a cut
1% – 3%10 ptsLow income value but lower risk of cuts
< 1%0 ptsNegligible income — not a dividend stock
💸
2. Payout Ratio
Worth up to 25 points
The payout ratio is the percentage of earnings paid out as dividends. A payout ratio of 0% scores zero points — a company paying no dividend at all is not a dividend investment regardless of its other qualities. A company paying out more than it earns cannot sustain the dividend long-term. REITs and Utilities are granted relaxed thresholds because they are legally required (REITs) or structurally expected to distribute most of their income.

81% – 100%5 ptsAll sectors (normal)
> 100%0 ptsAll sectors — paying more than earned
≤ 95%25 ptsREITs & Utilities only
96% – 110%15 ptsREITs & Utilities only
> 110%5 ptsREITs & Utilities only

3. Beta (Volatility)
Worth up to 20 points
Beta measures how much a stock moves relative to the market. A beta of 1.0 means it moves in lockstep with the S&P 500. Lower beta means a steadier, more predictable stock — important for income investors who can't afford large drawdowns. Stocks with unknown beta receive 10 points (neutral credit).
BetaPointsMeaning
≤ 0.520 ptsVery stable — moves much less than the market
0.51 – 0.817 ptsStable — typical defensive stock
0.81 – 1.013 ptsMarket-like — moderate volatility
1.01 – 1.38 ptsAbove average — moves more than the market
1.31 – 1.64 ptsHigh volatility — significant swings
> 1.60 ptsVery high — not typical for income stocks

4. EPS (Earnings Per Share)
Worth up to 15 points
EPS is the company's net profit divided by shares outstanding — a direct measure of profitability. A company must be earning money to sustain a dividend over the long run. We use trailing twelve-month (TTM) EPS so it reflects actual recent results, not forecasts. Unknown EPS receives 5 points rather than zero.

Negative0 ptsLosing money — dividend is at risk

5. Sector Reliability Bonus
Worth up to 15 points
Some sectors have structural advantages that make dividends more reliable — recession-proof demand, regulated monopolies, or legal distribution requirements. This bonus rewards stocks in historically dependable sectors. It is a blunt signal, not a guarantee: a tech company with a great track record can still score well on the other four factors.
